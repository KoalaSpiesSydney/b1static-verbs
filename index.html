Understanding State Verbs

State verbs describe feelings, thoughts, possession, senses, and existence, not actions. This is why "I don't believe you" is correct and "I'm not believing you" is weird and incorrect. Or why you say, "I'm hungry right now" and not "I'm being hungry right now" even though it is temporary situations and the second one makes sense with what we know about the continuous tense.

You can turn state verbs into gerunds/ nouns to be the subject of a sentence, "Believing in yourself is important" but not as a verb "I believe in myself" is correct in usual English.

On the rare occasion when state verbs are used in continuous tenses, it is usually for a unique context. For instance, "I need it." is normally correct English but if someone is desperate or angry they might scream, "I'm needing it!" It's important that you get the tone as well as the grammar right when language learning.


This lesson is about identifying state verbs so that you use them in simple tenses, which is correct almost all the time, unless they have a change of meaning or tone in a rare situation. When in doubt, use them in a simple tense to always be correct.
